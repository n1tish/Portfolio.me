Overview

Not a straight line — a deliberate climb.

Most product managers come from engineering or MBA programmes. I came from the queue — handling escalations, debugging broken Zaps at midnight, and sitting inside customer frustration long enough to understand it deeply.

That unusual path turned out to be a superpower. Every product decision I make is grounded in something I have personally felt: the friction of a confusing UI, the cost of missing SLA, the moment a support rep realises the product cannot do what the customer needs it to do.

"The best product instincts I have come not from frameworks, but from having spent years on the other side of them — watching users struggle in real time."

The arc

  • Started as a Systems Engineer supporting enterprise software across 40+ countries
  • Moved into product support at scale — 1,000+ tickets analysed across 6 B2B SaaS products
  • Joined Zapier as a Senior Product Support Engineer; operated at the boundary of product and engineering
  • Began filing product feedback, influencing roadmaps, and co-building AI-powered tooling
  • Made the transition to Senior Product Manager at Thena — owning 0→1 AI product development end-to-end
  • Became fluent in LLMs, RAG, agentic systems, and AI evaluation through direct product ownership
The Path

Key transitions

What each shift unlocked.

Support → Analyst

Shifted from solving individual tickets to identifying systemic issues. Learned to turn volume into signal — the difference between fixing a bug and understanding a category of failure.

Analyst → Engineer

Gained technical depth. Started reading code, understanding APIs, and speaking the language of engineering. This made me a better collaborator and a sharper bug filer — specificity matters.

Engineer → PM

Moved from influencing to owning. Took on full accountability for outcomes — strategy, prioritisation, delivery, and measurement. All the muscle memory from before paid dividends here.

The AI layer

How I became fluent in AI — not just adjacent to it.

Most product managers "work on AI products." I built them. That means writing prompts that actually hold up under adversarial input, designing RAG pipelines with the right chunking and retrieval strategy, defining evaluation rubrics that measure what matters, and debugging grounding failures at 2am before a demo.

The fluency is not theoretical. It comes from ownership — from being the person who has to explain to an enterprise customer why the AI gave a wrong answer, and then having to fix it.

AI capabilities built in production

  • AI copilot with RAG-grounded response generation
  • L1 deflection agent with per-channel knowledge scoping
  • Sentiment scoring and priority surfacing on inbound requests
  • AI evaluation framework: offline testing + in-product telemetry
  • AI ticket automation suite (summarisation, draft replies, troubleshooting)
  • AI recommendation engine for upsell opportunity detection
  • Prompt engineering, context window optimisation, failure mode mapping
Reflection

What the journey built in me.

Empathy
for the user, not just the metric

Years in support means I have felt the frustration firsthand. I do not need a persona document to tell me why a user might abandon a flow — I have been that user.

Speed
from problem to solution

Having operated across support, engineering, and product, I can move fast between context layers. I speak the language of every stakeholder in the room.

Rigour
in defining what "done" looks like

Every product I have shipped has a success metric attached. Not because someone asked for one — because I know from experience how easy it is to ship and never know if it worked.

Taste
for what AI should and should not do

Fluency in AI is not about using every model feature. It is knowing when automation creates leverage and when it creates noise — and having the data to tell the difference.
